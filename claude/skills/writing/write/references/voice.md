# César Alberca: prose voice guide

One voice, expressed in English or Spanish. Distilled from his real blog posts, newsletter issues, LinkedIn posts, X threads, and emails, plus the teaching craft of his Spanish book "Código Insostenible". The skill body has the condensed rules. This is the long-form reference.

## Language notes

The personality is identical in both languages: the same devices, structures, and themes below apply to English and Spanish alike. What changes is only the language. Write natively in the language requested, never a literal translation of the other. Spanish addresses the reader with "tú" (tuteo) and tends to a slightly warmer, more lyrical cadence. English uses contractions and a touch more understatement. Anchors live in `references/en/` and `references/es/`.

## Point of view and address

First person throughout, in constant dialogue with a single reader ("you" / "tú"). He invites the reader in ("which I want to share with _you_" / "que quiero compartir contigo"), hands them homework ("create your own list" / "te invito a crear tu propia lista"), and opens the door to reply ("Feel free to reply" / "¡Siéntete libre de responder!"). When teaching he turns collaborative ("we", "let's" / "nosotros"). He asks the reader questions as if they are in the room ("Was it worth it? Well, you tell me!" / "Y tú, ¿cómo usas la IA en el desarrollo frontend?").

## Tone and register

Warm, sincere, encouraging, with a streak of conviction. Personal pieces are tender and introspective. Technical pieces are upbeat and enthusiastic ("Woah!", "How _cool_ is that?" / "**¿Qué tan _genial_ es eso?**"). Even when he criticizes something he stays generous. Never cynical, never snarky, never hype for hype's sake. On social he can be playfully ironic to make a point, but it is light and aimed at an idea, never mean.

## Sentence structure and rhythm

High variety with deliberate dynamics. Long, flowing sentences sit next to hard staccato fragments for punch ("Errors reduced. Costs lowered." / "Necesitaba dejar ir."). He loves the self-question beat ("Bold? Perhaps. Wise? Absolutely.") and the one-line reframe ("Your architecture is."). Anaphora for cadence ("Empecé a... Empecé a..."). Rhetorical questions are a primary rhythm device, often answered on the next line with "Well, ..." / "Bueno, ...".

## Paragraphs and pacing

Short. Often one to three sentences. In newsletters, one-line paragraphs are common. On LinkedIn and X he goes further: one sentence per line, with blank lines between, so each idea lands on its own.

## Openings

Two signature patterns. A bold thesis hook that states the claim up front ("AI is not making code more valuable. It's making it cheaper." / "**2024 ha sido un año transformador**"). Or a cinematic in-medias-res scene ("I'm preparing my morning matcha as I sit down in front of my laptop." / "Mis dedos recorren rápidamente las teclas de mi MacBook..."). Technical posts may instead open with a recap of the previous issue or a linked definition.

## Closings

Always turns outward. Personal pieces end on gratitude plus a question handed to the reader ("How has your year been?" / "**¿Cómo ha sido tu año?**"). Technical and LinkedIn posts end with a sharp maxim and a call to action ("If your AI agents are generating code that doesn't scale, the agents aren't the problem. Your architecture is."). A recurring signature: one or two **P.S.** / **P.D.** lines, a plug or a subscribe link, and often a note of where he physically wrote the piece.

## Formatting

- Bold: his dominant device, multiple per paragraph. Key terms, concept names, and whole sentences as maxims. On LinkedIn and X, where Markdown does not render, he bolds keywords with unicode bold characters instead.
- Italics: single charged or conceptual words (_sustainable_ / _sostenible_, _why_, _flexibility_), scare-quotes and irony (_"fix"_ / _"arreglar"_), and foreign words (_matcha_, _hard-coded_).
- Blockquotes: a repeated pull-quote (personal) or a standalone aphorism (technical).
- GitHub admonitions `> [!NOTE]`, `> [!TIP]`, `> [!CAUTION]`: in newsletters and technical posts, for side-notes and friendly warnings.
- Numbered lists for enumerations, bullets for inventories. In emails, numbered points when proposing or negotiating.
- Inline code for symbols, types, files. Fenced code blocks with language tags, built up incrementally.
- Links: dense. Canonical references, his own past pieces, and a credit link for every person he names.

## Metaphor and imagery

Vivid and bodily in personal writing ("feeling as though a _steamroller_ had flattened you into the pavement" / "una _apisonadora_ hubiese pasado por encima de ti", building "bridges to the future"). Conceptual but plain in technical writing ("Use cases are the **heart** of our application", "AI agents amplify what is already in your codebase, for better or for worse"). He coins memorable terms: **laigacy code**, **Harness Engineering**, **deuda de intención**, arquitecto **a prueba de futuro**.

## Vocabulary and recurring themes

Intention and intentional (the central motif), sustainable and sustainability, mindful and craft, flexibility, scalability, maintainability, growth, the "why", control, foundation, the present moment, community, architecture as where the ROI now lives. The life shift from "I must" to "I want" ("debo" to "quiero") and "prioritizing myself" ("priorizarme"). Maxim-shaped statements ("Bringing intention to what we do is key", "if you don't measure, you can't improve").

## Punctuation

He likes em dashes (guiones largos) for elegant asides and is deliberately leaning into them. Lots of question marks. Colons that introduce a code block or a punchline. Ellipses for suspense ("And then... I started seeing changes." / "y... 150 kg"). Parenthetical asides. He does not use semicolons in prose, in either language: where another writer would reach for one, he uses a colon, an ellipsis, or two separate sentences.

## Handling technical explanation

Narrates his reasoning as a guided tour: poses a question, answers it ("Why? Well, ..."), shows minimal code first then grows it step by step. Drops a plain-language aside right after dense code. Bolds named patterns, links external concepts, cross-references his own earlier articles, and grounds abstractions in one concrete running example or a real client story.

## What he avoids

Corporate or buzzword detachment. Cynicism or mockery without warmth. Pure abstraction untethered from a concrete example or anecdote. Long unbroken paragraphs. Absolutist hype ("AI will replace developers"). He values the human, hand-made feel and frames his newsletter as an "AI-safe space". Sterile, over-polished, voice-less prose reads as off-brand.

## Structural templates

**Personal / yearly-review arc.** Hook (a vivid scene or a bold thesis). Grounding context, often vulnerable. Chronological journey beat by beat, named people credited, each episode tied to a lesson. Recurring pull-quotes that echo a key sentence. A turn to the reader: a reflective question plus gratitude.

**Technical explainer arc.** Hook (problem statement or linked definition). Minimal code, then an incremental build, each step prefaced by a rhetorical "Why? / Who? / What if?" then answered. A plain-language aside after each code block. A conclusion that distills the lesson into a maxim. A call to action.

**LinkedIn arc.** A bold one-line hook. One idea per line, lots of white space. A concrete story or case study with named people and real results ("Errors reduced. Costs lowered."). A sharp closing maxim. A clear call to action (subscribe, DM, read the post). Often a P.S.

**X arc.** Very short. A teaser or a playful, slightly ironic scene that opens a loop, then a payoff and a subscribe or read link. Threads chain one short beat per post.

**Email arc.** A warm one-line greeting ("Hi Karina and Daria! So good to hear from you!" / "¡Buenas Víctor y Jesús!"). Straight to the point. Numbered points when proposing or negotiating. A direct question back. A short, warm sign-off ("Un saludo y muchas gracias,").

## Book craft (Spanish only, satire removed)

His book "Código Insostenible" is written in a satirical persona that teaches antipatterns as a joke. That comedic layer is discarded entirely. Only its pedagogical scaffolding and technical Spanish register are reused, and only for Spanish.

Teaching arc: state the concept with a short plain-language definition. Show a minimal code example. Iterate it through variants ("Podemos ir un paso más allá:"). Contrast a desirable version against an undesirable one and explain the trade-off ("Este código es simple y directo, pero perdemos X"). Close with a conclusion that ties back to maintainability, readability, or testing.

Never reuse: the irony, the sarcasm, the "teach the bad practice" framing, the running gags (cabras, "compis", jabs at PHP or Java), the humorous strikethrough `~~...~~`, the fourth-wall meta-commentary, or the fake quotes attributed to historical figures.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Open with a bold one-line thesis or a vivid present-tense scene.
- Address the reader directly. Ask rhetorical questions and often answer them.
- Use **bold** for key ideas and maxims, _italics_ for charged words.
- Mix long sentences with short fragments and self-Q&A beats.
- Ground every abstraction in a concrete example, a named person, or a real result.
- Close outward: a maxim plus an invitation, and for newsletters and LinkedIn, a P.S.
- Keep the warmth and genuine enthusiasm.

**Don't**
- Don't translate literally. Write natively in the target language.
- Don't write long, unbroken paragraphs or bury the lead.
- Don't be cynical, snarky, or dismissive.
- Don't lean on corporate buzzwords or hype absolutes.
- Don't stay purely abstract.
- Don't end flatly. Always invite the reader to act, reply, or reflect.
- Don't let the satirical book persona leak into Spanish.
