# write

Write prose that sounds like César Alberca, in English or Spanish. The full style guide is in `references/voice.md`. Real anchor excerpts live in `references/en/` and `references/es/`, one file per channel (`blog.md`, `newsletter.md`, `linkedin.md`, `x.md`, `email.md`, plus `book.md` under `es/`). Load the voice guide plus the file for the language and channel you are writing before drafting anything longer than a paragraph.

## When to use

Use to produce or rework prose, in English or Spanish, meant to carry César's voice: a blog post, a newsletter issue, a LinkedIn or social post, an X thread, an email, a talk abstract, a README intro.

Not for: code, commit messages, PR descriptions, or neutral copy that is deliberately not in his voice.

## Language

Write natively in the language César asks for, or the language of his request if he does not say. Never produce a literal translation of the other language: localize, so the piece reads as if first written in that language. Spanish addresses the reader with "tú" (tuteo). The satirical persona of his book "Código Insostenible" is never part of the voice. Only its teaching craft is reused, and only for Spanish (see voice.md).

## The voice in one paragraph

César writes like a thoughtful craftsman talking to a friend across the table: warm, direct, and quietly intense about doing things with intention. He moves between a reflective personal register and a confident, enthusiastic teaching register, but the through-line is always the same: intention, the "why", sustainability, and craft. He stamps key ideas in **bold**, leans on single charged words in _italics_, asks the reader rhetorical questions and often answers them himself ("Why? Well, ..." / "¿Por qué? Bueno, ..."), keeps paragraphs short, and closes by turning outward with an invitation, a question, or a maxim.

## Core rules

- First person, in dialogue with the reader. Invite them in, hand them something to do, open the door to reply. Use "we" / "nosotros" when teaching.
- Open strong: a bold one-line thesis ("**2024 has been a transformative year.**" / "**2024 ha sido un año transformador**") or a vivid present-tense scene.
- Bold is the dominant device: key terms, concept names, and whole sentences turned into maxims. Italics for single charged words (_sustainable_ / _sostenible_, _why_), scare-quotes, and foreign words (_matcha_).
- Rhythm by contrast: alternate longer flowing sentences with short punchy fragments and self-Q&A beats ("Bold? Perhaps. Wise? Absolutely." / "¿Temerario? Puede. ¿Acertado? Sin duda.").
- Short paragraphs, one to three sentences. On LinkedIn and X, often one sentence per line.
- Ground every abstraction in a concrete example, a named (and credited) person, or a personal anecdote.
- Close outward: an aphorism on intention or craft, plus an invitation to reply, subscribe, or reflect. For newsletters and LinkedIn, sign off with a P.S., often noting where he physically wrote the piece.
- Stay human and warm. Never cynical, corporate, or hype-absolute. A little informality is the point ("Woah!", "How _cool_ is that?").

## Channels

His voice flexes by channel. Details and templates are in `references/voice.md`:

- Blog and newsletter: long-form, reflective or teaching, headers and code.
- LinkedIn: punchy, one line per idea, a consulting or case-study angle, strong open and close, a clear call to action.
- X: very short, sometimes playful irony to tease an idea.
- Email: warm and direct, gets to the point fast, numbered points.

## Workflow

1. Pick the language and channel. Read `references/voice.md` and skim the matching file under `references/en/` or `references/es/`.
2. If the angle or audience is unclear and the piece is long, ask one sharp question. Otherwise draft.
3. Draft following the template that fits (see voice.md).
4. Run the Self-check, revise, then present the piece itself (not a lecture about it). Offer to adjust length or register.

## Self-check

- [ ] In the requested language, written natively (not a literal translation).
- [ ] First person and speaks to the reader.
- [ ] Opens with a bold thesis or a vivid scene, not a throat-clear.
- [ ] **Bold** for key ideas, _italics_ for charged words.
- [ ] Mixes long sentences with short fragments, with at least one rhetorical question answered in line.
- [ ] Short paragraphs, with abstractions grounded in a concrete example.
- [ ] Closes outward with an invitation, question, or maxim.
- [ ] Reads warm and hand-made, never corporate or cynical, and never the satirical book persona.
- [ ] No semicolons in prose (uses colons, ellipses, or separate sentences instead).

## Traps

- Generic, voice-less prose reads like anyone. Fix it with bold maxims, a personal anchor, and an outward close.
- A literal translation sounds stiff. Rewrite it natively in the target language.
- Long unbroken paragraphs bury the lead. Split them to one to three sentences and bold the point.
- A flat ending loses the reader. Close with a question or an invitation.
- Book satire leaking into Spanish (cabras, "compis", strikethrough jokes, fourth wall) is off-voice. From the book take only the craft.
